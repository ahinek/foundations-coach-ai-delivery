# Responsibilities

These responsibilities describe how the core principles show up in practice.

They are not additional layers of authority.  
They are observable behaviors that help ensure AI accelerates healthy systems rather than fragmenting them.

---

## Steward tooling coherence

AI experimentation often expands faster than coordination.

This responsibility includes:

- Encouraging shared understanding of which AI tools are in use  
- Surfacing integration risks introduced by divergent workflows  
- Promoting agreed-upon review and quality standards  
- Helping teams converge on coherent tooling ecosystems  

The aim is not uniformity for its own sake.  
It is reducing hidden complexity and integration friction.

---

## Protect collaborative rhythms

AI can inadvertently encourage isolated "productivity".

This responsibility includes:

- Reinforcing shared planning and review practices  
- Ensuring stand-ups remain alignment conversations, not status reporting  
- Encouraging early integration rather than late reconciliation  
- Watching for patterns of parallel work that weaken cohesion  

Acceleration should strengthen teamwork, not replace it.

---

## Surface product clarity gaps

Speed exposes ambiguity.

This responsibility includes:

- Helping teams articulate what “good” looks like before building faster  
- Identifying when output increases but clarity decreases  
- Encouraging visible definitions of value, usability, and success  
- Connecting experimentation back to strategic intent  

Acceleration amplifies direction. Direction must be explicit.

---

## Encourage measurable experimentation

AI experimentation benefits from intention.

This responsibility includes:

- Defining in advance what a given AI experiment aims to improve  
- Establishing measurable stop/go thresholds  
- Revisiting experiments to evaluate actual impact  
- Preventing open-ended drift under the banner of innovation  

Learning compounds when evaluation is structured.

---

## Translate system signals upward

Acceleration changes system dynamics.

This responsibility includes:

- Surfacing emerging risks before they become systemic  
- Communicating patterns of fragmentation or integration strain  
- Helping leadership understand trade-offs between speed and coherence  
- Advocating for a sustainable pace when acceleration pressures rise  

Stewardship requires visibility across levels.

---

## Reinforce shared accountability

AI does not remove responsibility.

This responsibility includes:

- Clarifying that generated work still carries human ownership  
- Encouraging teams to understand, not just accept, AI-generated outputs  
- Protecting quality standards under delivery pressure  
- Ensuring accountability remains collective rather than isolated  

Acceleration does not absolve ownership.

---

These responsibilities are most effective when integrated into existing roles — particularly Scrum Masters, Agile Coaches, Engineering Managers, and Delivery Leaders.

They require room to observe, guide, and align.

They do not require control.

They require stewardship.
